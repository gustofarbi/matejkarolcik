# Ecce Homo

I am sending this to you because you are the people who make up my world, yet for a long time, none of you have truly known who I am. That is entirely on me. I have been an expert at hiding—my mistakes, my addictions, my tastes, and my opinions.

But if I am being completely honest, **what I am most ashamed of, and what I have guarded most fiercely, is my deep inability to be intimate and the immense shame I have carried around my sexuality.**

The following lines are a confession. I claim full responsibility for my mistakes. My goal is not to lay blame on anyone, but to step out of the shadows, face my deepest vulnerabilities, and finally be seen.

### The Architecture of Hiding

Hiding became second nature to me when I was very young, growing up in a small Eastern European town. My childhood baseline was defined by fear. My father’s preferred method of parenting was verbal abuse—being yelled at, called stupid, and threatened. I learned to survive by ensuring my "no" never counted. When things I loved were arbitrarily taken away from me, I learned a dangerous lesson: if you care about something, hide it, or it will be used against you.

I was no angel. I stole about 100 euros from him to buy sweets for my classmates, desperate to buy the popularity and acceptance I wasn't getting at home. When I couldn't face the bullies at judo practice, I lied about going and spent freezing winter days walking outside just to avoid being home alone with my father before my mother got back. I learned to mask my terror and isolate myself early on.

### The Core Shame: Sex and Intimacy

Because of a very distorted view of intimacy at home, I developed an early coping mechanism through porn. It became a substitute for real connection before I even knew what real connection was.

By the time I was a teenager, lifting weights and getting attention from girls, the inner critic in my head was fully formed. **I knew for a fact—or so I thought—how disgusting I was as a person.** Because I felt fundamentally defective, the idea of a girl getting close enough to see the real me terrified me.

This deep-seated anxiety governed my entire youth:

* **The Fear of Vulnerability:** I knew several girls had crushes on me, but I never could follow through. I had to be cautious not to show myself. If they found out who I really was, I would be destroyed.
* **My First Experience:** At 18, right before leaving for university in Munich, I had my first sexual encounter with a prostitute. I didn't even realize she was one at first when she approached me. I initially said no, but turned back because I was desperate to just get the milestone over with. It was neither magical nor special, and I was just glad to have crossed the threshold.
* **Using Substances to Connect:** As an adult, alcohol became my way to override the screaming voice of self-hatred in my head. I adopted a casual "whatever, I'm not interested" attitude toward women just to avoid the risk of failing or being exposed.
* **The Four Occasions:** Throughout my twenties, I only managed to sleep with someone a total of four times, and I had to be completely drunk to do it. Whenever I didn't get drunk enough, or if I started to sober up in bed next to a girl, the panic would set in. I would pretend to be sleepy, claim I didn't have a condom, or make up an excuse to go home.

I hid all of this because admitting to a lack of romantic or sexual confidence felt like the ultimate weakness. I even lied to people and said I was dating a girl I went climbing with, simply because I was too ashamed to admit I had never had a girlfriend.

### Lifelines and Turning Points

I would not be here without the people who threw me lifelines when I was drowning in this isolation:

* **Herr Nicolini:** My German teacher, who was the first male role model to tell me not to beat myself up over mistakes. I am ashamed I never visited him after leaving Slovakia. Thank you for your patience.
* **The Guys at Work in Germany:** You showed me what real men look like—imperfect, but owning their errors.
* **My Aunt and Uncle (Matthias and Klaudia):** Thank you for showing me that a family can function with love, without fear, and in total safety. I never felt more at home than in Ebersberg with you.

### Open Apologies and Restitution

To live in the truth, I must clear my conscience regarding the moments where my unresolved pain and stress caused me to hurt others:

* **To my sister, Rasto, Roman, and Juro:** At around 25, drowning in financial stress from university fees and struggling to find a job, I hit a breaking point. I got dangerously drunk, had a massive panic attack, and tried to fight a statue in Trnava. I was aggressive, and I deeply apologize to everyone who had to witness that. Thank you for supporting me when I was at my lowest.
* **To Roman:** I am so deeply sorry for punching you in Šaštín. I should have asked you how you were doing instead of reacting with violence.
* **To Katarina:** You were a great friend. I ended our friendship abruptly without explanation. Knowing you grew up without parental support, what I did was cruel. I regret it deeply.

### Behold the Man

To my family: I love you, and you make me truly happy. I am working every day to learn how to show it more openly, without fear.

To my fiancée: You inspire me to be a better man. I would have never possessed the courage to begin this journey into the light, or to face these deep sexual anxieties, without you. Watching you dance is the greatest joy of my life.

I am no longer running from my shadow. I am ready to talk, to hear you, and to stop hiding.
