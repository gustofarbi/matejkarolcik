I am sending this to you because you are the people who make up my world, and yet for a long time, none of you have truly known who I am. That is entirely on me. I have been an expert at hiding — my mistakes, my addictions, my tastes, my opinions. But if I am being completely honest, what I am most ashamed of, and what I have guarded most fiercely, is my deep inability to be intimate, and the immense shame I have carried around my sexuality.

What follows is a confession. I claim full responsibility for my mistakes. My goal is not to lay blame on anyone, but to step out of the shadows, face my deepest vulnerabilities, and finally be seen.

Hiding became second nature to me when I was very young. My childhood baseline was fear. My father's preferred method of parenting was verbal abuse — being yelled at, called stupid, threatened. I learned to survive by making sure my "no" never counted. When things I loved were arbitrarily taken away from me, I learned a dangerous lesson: if you care about something, hide it, or it will be used against you.

I was no angel. I stole about 100 euros from him to buy sweets for my classmates, desperate to buy the popularity and acceptance I wasn't getting at home. When I couldn't face the bullies at judo practice, I lied about going and spent freezing winter days walking outside, just to avoid being home alone with my father before my mother got back. I learned to mask my terror and isolate myself early on.

Because of a very distorted view of intimacy at home, I found an early coping mechanism in porn. It became a substitute for real connection before I even knew what real connection was. By the time I was a teenager, lifting weights and getting attention from girls, the inner critic in my head was fully formed. I knew for a fact — or so I thought — how disgusting I was as a person. And because I felt fundamentally defective, the idea of a girl getting close enough to see the real me terrified me.

That fear governed my entire youth. I knew several girls had crushes on me, but I could never follow through. I had to be careful not to show myself — if they found out who I really was, I would be destroyed. At 18, right before leaving for university in Munich, I had my first sexual encounter with a prostitute. I didn't even realize she was one at first when she approached me. I initially said no, but turned back, because I was desperate to just get the milestone over with. It was neither magical nor special. I was simply glad to have crossed the threshold.

As an adult, alcohol became my way to override the screaming voice of self-hatred in my head. I adopted a casual "whatever, I'm not interested" attitude toward women just to avoid the risk of failing or being exposed. Throughout my twenties, I only managed to sleep with someone a total of four times, and I had to be completely drunk to do it. Whenever I didn't get drunk enough, or started to sober up in bed next to a girl, the panic would set in. I would pretend to be sleepy, claim I didn't have a condom, make up an excuse to go home.

I hid all of this because admitting to a lack of romantic or sexual confidence felt like the ultimate weakness. I even lied and told people I was dating a girl I went climbing with, simply because I was too ashamed to admit I had never had a girlfriend.

I would not be here without the people who threw me lifelines while I was drowning in that isolation. Herr Nicolini, my German teacher, was the first male role model who told me not to beat myself up over mistakes — I am ashamed I never visited him after leaving Slovakia, and I thank him for his patience. The guys I worked with in Germany showed me what real men look like: imperfect, but owning their errors. And my aunt and uncle, Matthias and Klaudia, showed me that a family can function with love, without fear, in total safety. I never felt more at home than in Ebersberg with you.

To live in the truth, I also need to clear my conscience about the moments where my unresolved pain spilled onto others. To my sister, Rasto, Roman, and Juro: at around 25, drowning in financial stress from university fees, struggling to find a job, and unable to ask for help, I hit a breaking point. I got dangerously drunk, blacked out, had a panic attack, and tried to fight a statue in Trnava. I was aggressive, and I deeply apologize to everyone who had to witness that. Thank you for supporting me when I was at my lowest. To Roman: I am so deeply sorry for punching you in Šaštín. I should have asked you if anything was wrong instead of reacting with violence. And to Katarina: you were a great friend, and I ended our friendship abruptly, without explanation. Knowing what you went through growing up, what I did was cruel, and I regret it deeply.

To my family: I love you, and you make me truly happy. I am working every day to learn how to show it more openly, without fear.

To my fiancée: you inspire me to be a better man. I would never have had the courage to begin this journey into the light, or to face these anxieties, without you. Watching you dance is the greatest joy of my life.

I am no longer running from my shadow. I am ready to talk, to hear you, and to stop hiding.
